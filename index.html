A Return to Nanjing, After Loss

A Photo Essay

On January 22, 2023, Emma and I boarded a flight to China. It was my first trip back since the pandemic began. I carried my compact digital camera and decided to shoot only in black and white in Nanjing, my childhood home.

My mother passed away a month earlier during the devastating surge that began in December 2022 after the abrupt end of zero-COVID restrictions. Within weeks, nearly everyone I knew in China has been infected; countless elders, including my mother, did not survive. My father had died two years before. Travel bans and border closures meant I could not be there at either of their bedsides. I never got to say goodbye. Now, as one of the first “foreigners” to re-enter China after the lockdown, I was returning not to reunite, but to reckon with absence.

❧❧❧

My mother developed a persistent fever on November 25, 2022. I was half a world away at a Thanksgiving gathering that evening, anxiously watching through a video camera installed in her bedroom. She died two weeks later in the hospital.

When we arrived, their beds were empty. My father once slept on the bed to the right. My mother, worn down by Parkinson’s, needed her own.

Instead of greeting us at home, they were waiting for us at their final resting place.
Sifting through their belongings was overwhelming.
Among them, a local England newspaper from 1980: my father, second from the left, attending an international cultural event at Cranfield Institute of Technology, where he spent two years as a visiting scholar. A handwritten will in an old envelope. Airline napkins carefully saved from their visits to Texas, where their son, daughter-in-law, and grandchildren lived. Those visits were among the happiest moments in their lives.
Both my parents were professors. I grew up on the university campus, knowing every nook and cranny of it. Once vibrant, now it felt eerily frozen, emptied of life.
Entry to the campus was restricted. I was denied entry at first. Emma wouldn’t give up. She grabbed a pot from home and posed as a staff member heading to the cafeteria on campus. The security personnel was fooled and let us in.
We spent the last days walking, saying farewell to the old city.
Amid the stillness, I found comfort in the sight of young men shooting hoops in the shadow of the ancient City Wall.
Life continues, even after so much loss.
