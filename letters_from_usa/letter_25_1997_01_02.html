It Suits Me Well

Austin, TX
January 2, 1997
Dear Dad and Mom, Winter break is already half over, and I haven’t really accomplished much. I spent most of the first week practicing driving, and I actually managed to pass my road test and get my driver's license on December 23rd, just two days before Christmas. By law, before you get your license, you aren't allowed to drive alone-you have to have someone with at least a year of driving experience sitting next to you. But now that I have my license, I can drive by myself. I’ve been driving almost every day—going to campus, catching a movie, or running out for groceries. Learning how to drive itself isn't really that difficult; the main thing is gaining experience on the road, which simply takes time. Life is definitely a whole lot better now that I can drive. I have so much more freedom, and getting around is very convenient. I still tend to sleep late and wake up late, but I always make sure to get plenty of rest—probably more than most people around me. Last semester, I ate out about half the time and cooked the other half, but since break started, I’ve barely eaten out at all. When I do grab food on the go, it’s usually Mexican fast food like Taco Bell, or burgers and pizza, mostly just to save time. My own cooking isn't bad at all, though I rely heavily on various store-bought sauces; without them, my food probably wouldn't taste as good. Most of these sauces are imported from Hong Kong or Taiwan and may not be available back home in mainland. So even if I cook for you when I visit home, it might not taste as good as I've boasted! My new place is pretty nice, and the rent is on the cheaper side—about $230 a month. It’s significantly farther from campus than my old apartment, but it only takes about 15 minutes to drive there. The campus shuttles run very frequently too—one comes by every few minutes—so commuting is still a breeze. The neighborhood is in Southwest Austin, right near Lake Austin, and the surroundings are really beautiful. Time is flying by so fast over here; I’ve already been in the States for a year and four months. All things considered, it’s been a wonderful experience. I've adapted to life here and really enjoy it. One of the things I appreciate most about America is the sense of freedom—you can basically live your life as you please, and nobody bothers you, as long as you aren't breaking the law, of course. People here are very polite, law-abiding, and friendly, and society feels orderly and well run. You rarely see people shouting at each other in public, let alone getting into fights. It's a well-organized society, and I think it suits me very well. In the US, it’s much easier for Computer Science and EE (Electrical Engineering) majors to find work. Companies of all sizes come to campus to interview students. That said, more than half of these companies won't sponsor international students. Because of that, our options as international students are much more limited compared to Americans or green card holders. Even so, the vast majority of STEM graduate students are international anyway, mostly from India and China. Last semester, I did over a dozen interviews on campus. For one on-site interview in New York, a classmate came with me, but unfortunately he didn’t end up getting an offer. We stayed right on Broadway in a large hotel that cost over $140 a night. The interview site was only a five-minute walk from the hotel. We checked in around 7:00 on Friday evening and checked out early the next morning. Because of the unfamiliar environment, I didn’t sleep well that night, and the interview wrapped up around noon. Afterward, my classmate and I took a cab down to Chinatown. We walked around desperately looking for a restroom but couldn’t find one anywhere. We finally found one inside a Chinese restaurant, so we ended up eating lunch there—it was surprisingly cheap and very good. Chinatown doesn’t have much besides Chinese restaurants—some quite large—as well as a few grocery stores. There are street vendors along the sidewalks, and the streets were packed with Chinese people. It felt like walking down Wangfujing in Beijing, except almost everyone was speaking Cantonese. We flew back to Austin that same night. For another company in Austin, the on-site interview lasted an entire day—I went in around 8:00 a.m. and didn’t get out until 5:00 p.m. I’ll probably have another dozen or so interviews next semester, but I’m going to be much more selective this time. Interviewing takes up a lot of time, so I’ll stick to the companies I’m genuinely interested in. Next semester, I’m taking two courses outside my department, and once school starts, I need to stop by the department office to look into the paperwork for my master’s degree. I can’t believe how expensive postage has gotten in China—more than doubling! I wonder how much the price of everything else has shot up. Have salaries gone up to match? What are your monthly salaries looking like these days? I've seen some articles online claiming that the average salary in places like Guangzhou, Shanghai, and Beijing has already broken into the four-digit range, but I don’t know how accurate that is. You can't believe how much Chinese content I can find on the internet now. Wishing you all the best for 1997! Tong 01/01/97
