It Suits Me Well

Austin, TX
January 2, 1997
Dear Dad and Mom, Winter break is already half over, and I haven’t really gotten much done. I spent most of the first week practicing driving, and I actually managed to pass my road test and get my driver's license on December 23rd, just two days before Christmas. By law, before you get your actual license, you aren't allowed to drive alone; you have to have someone next to you who has been driving for at least a year. But now that I have it, I can drive by myself. I’ve been driving out basically every day now—going to campus, movies, or running out for groceries. Mastering driving skill itself isn't really that difficult; the main thing is just building up experience on the road, which takes time. Life is definitely a whole lot better now that I can drive. I have so much more freedom, and getting around is very convenient. My new place is pretty nice, and the rent is on the cheaper side—about $230 a month. It’s significantly farther from campus than my old apartment, but it only takes about 15 minutes to drive there. The campus shuttles run very frequently too—one comes by every few minutes—so commuting is still a breeze. The neighborhood is in Southwest Austin, right near Lake Austin, and the surroundings are really beautiful. I can’t believe how expensive postage has gotten in China—more than doubling! I wonder how much the price of everything else has shot up. Have salaries gone up to match? What are your monthly salaries looking like these days? I've seen some articles online claiming that the average salary in places like Guangzhou, Shanghai, and Beijing has already broken into the four-digit range, but I don’t know how accurate that is. It's amazing how much Chinese content you can find on the internet now. Time is flying by so fast over here; I’ve already been in the States for a year and four months. All things considered, it’s been great. I've adapted to life here and really enjoy it. One of the best things about America is the sense of freedom—you can basically do whatever you want and nobody bothers you, as long as you aren't breaking the law, of course. People here are very polite, law-abiding, and friendly, and society just feels orderly and well-run. You never see people shouting at each other in public, let alone getting into fights. It's a well-organized society, and I think it suits me very well. I still tend to sleep late and wake up late, but I always make sure to get plenty of rest—probably more than most people around me. Last semester, I ate out about half the time and cooked the other half, but since break started, I’ve barely eaten out at all. When I do grab food on the go, it’s usually Mexican fast food like Taco Bell, or burgers and pizza, mostly just to save time. My own cooking isn't bad at all, though I rely heavily on various store-bought sauces; without them, my food probably wouldn't taste as good. Most of these sauces are imported from Hong Kong or Taiwan and may not be available back home in mainland. So even if I cook for you when I visit home, it might not taste as good as I've boasted! In the US, it’s much easier for Computer Science and EE (Electrical Engineering) majors to find work. Companies of all sizes come to campus to interview students. That said, more than half of these companies won't sponsor international students. Because of that, our options as international students are much more limited compared to Americans or green card holders. Even so, the vast majority of STEM graduate students are international anyway, mostly from India and China. Last semester, I did over a dozen interviews on campus. Each one lasts about half an hour, and if a company is interested, they’ll invite you for a second-round interview, which usually takes place at their headquarters. When I went out to New York for my interview, another classmate came along with me, but unfortunately, he didn't end up getting the offer. We stayed right on Broadway in a big hotel that cost over $140 a night. The interview site was only a 5-minute walk from the hotel. We landed around 7:00 or 8:00 on Friday night and checked out early the next morning. Because of the unfamiliar environment, I didn't sleep well that night, and the interview wrapped up around noon. Afterward, my classmate and I took a cab down to Chinatown. We walked all over the place desperately looking for a restroom but couldn't find one anywhere! We finally found one inside a Chinese restaurant, so we just ended up eating lunch there—it was surprisingly cheap and really good. Chinatown doesn't have much else besides Chinese restaurants—some of them quite large—along with a few grocery stores. There are street vendors along the sidewalks, and the streets were packed with Chinese people. It felt like walking down Wangfujing in Beijing, except almost everyone was speaking Cantonese. We flew back to Austin that same night. For that other company here in Austin, the interview took an entire day—I went in around 8:00 AM and didn't get out until 5:00 PM. I’ll probably have another dozen or so interviews next semester, but I’m going to be much more selective this time around. Interviewing takes up a lot of time, so I’ll just stick to the companies I’m genuinely interested in. Next semester I’m taking two classes outside my department, and once school starts, I need to stop by the department office to look into the paperwork for my Master’s degree. I'll wrap things up here. Wishing you all the best for 1997! Tong 01/01/97
